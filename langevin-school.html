A School By Any Other Name

What was the name of a Quebecois Laywer Doing on a Calgary School Anyways?

June 1, 2021

The recent discovery of an unmarked grave at a residential school in Kamloops re-ignited discussions (and now action) about the naming of Langevin School in the Bridgeland-Riverside neighborhood of Calgary. If you're familiar with the story, you're probably familiar with the name Langevin and its connection to the residential school system in Canada. You might remember the 2018 renaming of a bridge by the same name, and for the same reason. Maybe these stories make you curious as to who this man Hector Langevin was. An once your curiosity gets the better of you and you look this fellow up, you're going to have the same question I did:

Why on Earth was anything in Calgary named after this guy in the first place?

And I don't mean this in the historical revisionist sense, as in, "how could anyone have honored this person given what they were responsible for?" What I mean is "how did a school on the Prairies come to be named after an ardent French nationalist?"

Yes, in addition to being an architect of the residential school system and its horrendous consequences, Sir Hector Langevin was one of the "fathers of confederation", a group of men who formed the first European self-ruling government within the Dominion of Canada. But there were many other such "fathers". What was special about Hector Langevin? Why Langevin School and not Macdonald School, after the "father" responsible for punching a rail system through the country, a system critical for the existence of Calgary as a permament settlement. The answer, it seems, is pretty innocent, even if the man himself was far from innocent in his practices and beliefs.

It's difficult to find a definite source, however both the Calgary Board of Education and a 1938 article appear to agree that Langevin School was so named in honor of the man, but primarily for the simple reason that the school is in the immediate vicinity of the Langevin bridge (the same bridge mentioned above)1. Obviously this just moves the question down the timeline. Why was the bridge named after Langevin? And it turns out the answer to that question, is another question: "Which Langevin bridge?"

The current steel structure crossing the Bow, now known as Reconciliation Bridge, dates to 1910. Langevin himself died in 1906 so maybe the newly constructed bridge was to honor his legacy, right? A legacy which would naturally involve his responsibility for the residential school system. But, again, Langevin was an ardent supporter for Quebec inside the confederation and this was 1910; it didn't take four years to build a bridge back then. Was his impact on this dusty Prairie town really so strong that a modern Steel bridge would bear his name, half a decade after his death? The answer is "probably not", and the simple explanation for this bridge's name is that it replaced a wooden structure on the same spot and by the same name.

Again we move the question down the timeline... So why was that bridge named after Langevin?

The original Langevin bridge was a timber crossing which had been constructed in the 1880s, only a decade or so after Calgary was established as a settlement2. It had been "completed at a very considerable cost" with funding from the federal government3,4. Over the years it went through various stages of repair and disrepair until it was eventually replaced with a steel bridge. Just like its successor, it crossed the Bow at what became 4th street East. The source of this federal money is documented in a contemporary article from the Herald and confirmed in later editions:

Sir Hector's Visit

Father LaCombe and Col Herchimer are in receipt of telegrams from Sir Hector Langevin, in which he places the time of his visit to Calgary about September 5th. The citizens intend [to entertain] Sir Hector in a manner that will be appreciated by him. ... A number of local improvements (badly needed) will be brought to his notice, a bridge across the Bow, Court house and Jail and a Protestant Cemetery are among the number"

- Calgary Herald, August 27, 1884 (page 1)

The reason Langevin School is so named is thus rather straight-forward: he was the man who some brought some money to the backwaters of the Dominion to build a bridge. So while we should celebrate the removal of this man and his legacy from our public lives and mourn the tragedy he helped form in our society, maybe we can also understand the original naming as an innocent tip-of-the-hat to the man who brought some pork to town.

1. Calgary Daily Herald, Oct 29, 1938, page 2 (the article seems to confuse Hector Langevin with Archbishop Adélard Langevin)

2. Calgary Daily Herald, Oct 20, 1910

3. Calgary Daily Herald, Oct 17 1890, page 1

4. Calgary Herald, October 14, 1891, page 4

5. In 1885 the council "sent a petition to Hector Langevin, then minister of the Interior, [regarding his promise to] build a bridge over the Bow" Calgary Daily Herald, July 3 1909, page 12
